I assigned some homework in one of the courses I'm currently teaching — it's called Artistic Realization in Digital Environments. My students were asked to find digital role models — not to focus on the content, but on *how* they do it. How they communicate things. The framework for communication.

One student whose primary interest was writing shared a site and said: "Maggie Appleton — her art and interests don't particularly speak to me, but the website itself is roughly what I want to achieve." The site was https://maggieappleton.com/. I immediately realized this was something out of the ordinary. Every time a student submitted something, I'd do a little extra research to see what framework they'd used — WordPress, Shopify, Squarespace, or whatever. Every example except Maggie's was built on a commercial platform. Her site was one where someone had sat down and thought: "How do I actually want this?" — ignored the templates and just built. From their own needs, from "this is how I'd want it to be."

And that made me realize how deeply we're stuck inside other people's [[businesschmodels.en|business models]]. We're stuck because they've convinced us this is the only way — that it's so much easier. Which I firmly believe is demonstrably not true, especially now with the help of AI.

I kept reading Maggie's site. I read her essay on [digital gardens](https://maggieappleton.com/garden-history). I was almost furious — mostly at myself. How the hell had I missed this? How had this passed me by? Why was I not invited to this party?

Because it put its finger on exactly what I've felt has been missing from the web. Like many people, I've completely lost faith in social media. Social media is about selling your content to someone else, being entirely at the mercy of someone else's business model. Where you have zero control and zero options unless you're incredibly compliant and constantly trying to hack the algorithm to go viral.

I don't need my posts to go viral. Maybe my content reaches the right people. And maybe I'm not in a rush.

But also: the standard advice when starting a social media channel — when you're supposed to grab your megaphone and broadcast to the masses — tip number one is always to find a concept. Talk about one thing only. You can't talk about complex systems, politics, *and* disc golf or tabletop RPGs. You have to pick. You have to specialize. And I've always felt: "Really? Is that necessary? What a shame." Because with my ADHD brain, everything connects. It's all part of the same cloud of concepts that always relate to each other.

I've been using Obsidian for many, many years. And just that — writing in Obsidian, in that format — makes my brain go "mmm, lovely." It unlocked an enormous amount of creativity in me, an incredible accessibility and closeness to creative thought, by stepping away from a linear format.

The conclusion, in brief — digital gardening was made for me.

Whether this becomes yet another graveyard on the internet where letters and words slowly fade and eventually end up in the Wayback Machine — time will tell.
