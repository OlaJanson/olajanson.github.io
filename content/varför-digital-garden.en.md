I assigned a homework in one of the courses I'm currently teaching. It's called artistic realization in digital environments. My students were tasked with finding digital role models — but not focusing on the content, but on *how* they do it. How they present things. The framework for communication.

A student whose primary interest was in writing shared a page and said: "Maggie Appleton's, whose art/interests I'm not particularly interested in, but the website itself is roughly what I want to achieve.” The site the student shared was https://maggieappleton.com/. I immediately realized that this was something out of the ordinary. For example, every time a student submitted something, I did a little extra research to see what framework the person had used — WordPress, Shopify, Squarespace, or whatever the hell they use. And all examples except Maggie's page were made using commercial frameworks. Her page was where someone had sat down and thought "how do I want it?" — disregarded templates, and just went for it. Built from their own needs, from "this is how I would like it to be".

And that made me realize: how much we are stuck in others' [[businesschmodels|business models]]. We are stuck because they have convinced us that this is the only way to do it — that it's so much simpler. And I believe that is demonstrably not true, especially not now with the help of AI.

I read more on Maggie's page. I read her article on [digital gardens](https://maggieappleton.com/garden-history). I almost got angry, but mostly at myself — how the hell did I miss this? How did this pass me by? Why was I not invited to this party? 

It pinpointed exactly what I've felt has been missing right now in web contexts. I feel, like many, that my trust in social media has been completely lost. Social media is about selling your content to someone else and being entirely at the mercy of someone else's business model. Where you have zero control or zero opportunities to do anything if you're not incredibly compliant and constantly trying to hack the code to go viral.

I don't need my posts to go viral. Maybe just for my content to reach the right people. And perhaps I'm not in a hurry either. 

But also: the advice if you're going to start a channel on some social media, where you're going to start cranking up the megaphone and speaking out to the masses — advice number one is always to find a concept. Talk about only one thing. You can't talk about complex systems, politics, and disc golf or role-playing games. It's not possible. You have to choose, you have to be specialized. And [[jag|I]] am broad and want to talk about everything. I've always felt: "damn, do you have to do that? What a shame." Because with my ADHD brain, everything is connected. It's part of the same cloud of concepts that are always related to each other. 

I've been using Obsidian for many, many years now. And just that — writing in Obsidian, in that format — makes my brain just "mm, lovely". It unleashed a lot of creativity in me, an incredible amount of immediate and easily accessible creativity, by moving away from a linear format.

The conclusion in short – digital gardening is made for me. 

Then I don't know if it will just become another graveyard on the internet where letters and words wither away and eventually end up in the Wayback Machine. Time will tell.
