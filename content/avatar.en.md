##### Unconditional love

I'm sitting with my 10-year-old doing our Saturday binge mornings of Avatar the Last Airbender. Or, we've gotten as far as the end of the season finale of the second season of Legend of Korra.

Avatar the Last Airbender, the animated series from Nickelodeon, is a 10/10 in my eyes. I can't recall a single episode that made me doze off and lose focus. This also applies to my children, all four of them I'd say, despite them watching the entire series at completely different times. If you're a parent with children approaching their tenth birthday, this is a perfect introduction to next-level storytelling. It's incredibly satisfying to hear your children ask you at the beginning "Is he evil?" and be able to smile inwardly and cryptically answer "we'll see…". This, with the knowledge that you have introduced them to a tradition of storytelling with complex characters with depth and many layers.

Legend of Korra was not as impressive at first. A bit too much silly, a bit too much soap opera romance. The craftsmanship was masterful, but perhaps a slightly too large overdose, or rather, a multitude of spectacular powers. I became worried that what was masterful in season one had turned into a formula that was being mechanically applied. But halfway through season two, it grew and rose. It rose to such a degree that I'm now seriously considering canceling the whole day of brilliant sunshine and saying we're staying indoors to watch the rest.[^1]

If I were to pick one thing that both The Last Airbender and Legend of Korra do immensely well, it's character development. Characters that we would typically describe as archetypes for good or evil are allowed to be taken along loooong arcs to eventually end up in a place where you secretly always wished they were. Another hallmark of both is the choice of characters that truly develop. Don't get me wrong, most characters in the ensemble undergo some form of development, but some of the characters are taken on a much more emotionally strong journey. What's special is that it's not about the core group in the ensemble, but side characters who are certainly important but perhaps don't always play a central role in every episode. In The Last Airbender, Prince Zuko is the absolute strongest example of this, but in Legend of Korra, I would say it's Tenzin. Perhaps it's because, just like me, he is a father to many children. But I was truly touched by the burden of responsibility as the last of his kind and the burden of parenthood.

I say like Eska:
*"You will always hold a special place in the organ that pumps my blood.”*
